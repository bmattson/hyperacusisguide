Tympanic Membrane Paper Patching

Tympanic membrane paper patching is a minimally invasive procedure that has been explored as a treatment for hyperacusis, particularly in patients who experience excessive sensitivity or pain from sound due to abnormal vibration or hypermobility of the eardrum. The technique involves placing a small piece of sterile paper, cigarette paper, or similar thin material over the tympanic membrane to slightly stiffen the eardrum and reduce its vibratory response to incoming sound waves. By dampening eardrum movement, the paper patch may help lessen the mechanical transmission of sound energy to the ossicles and inner ear, thereby reducing loudness perception and discomfort. Some patients have reported immediate or gradual improvement in sound tolerance following the procedure, with minimal risks or complications. In certain cases, the patch is used as a diagnostic tool—if it provides temporary relief, more permanent options such as cartilage reinforcement or Silverstein round/oval window reinforcement may be considered. Although clinical data are limited, tympanic membrane patching is valued for its simplicity, reversibility, and potential to provide relief for patients with mechanically mediated hyperacusis or noxacusis-like pain linked to middle ear hypersensitivity.
